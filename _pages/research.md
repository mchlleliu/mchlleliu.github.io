# **What are the consequences of sex?**

Many sexual systems exhibit anisogamy, in which reproduction occurs through the fusion of two gametes that differ in size. This asymmetry in gamete investment leads to differences in how individuals producing smaller gametes (males) and larger gametes (females) maximize reproductive fitness. The resulting sex-specific selective pressures can cause the evolution of sexual dimorphism in morphology, physiology, behaviour, regulatory traits, and even in the structure of genomes (e.g., the heteromorphic sex chromosomes). My current work focuses in particular on the effects of sex-specific selection on transcriptomic variation within and across species.

<br>

### **The evolution of regulatory dimorphism**

Like other complex trait, expression traits can exhibit sexual dimorphism. Sex-biased gene expression (i.e., sexual dimorphism in transcript abundances) has been documented in many taxa, but transcriptomic dimorphism can also arise through sex-specific alternative splicing. 
- How common are sex-biased expression and sex-specific splicing across tissues? 
- Are these patterns typically consistent across tissues or highly context-dependent? 
- How often do sex-biased expression and sex-specific splicing co-occur within the same gene? 
- How do genes with dimorphism in expression levels and/or in splicing differ evolutionarily from genes that show no dimorphism?


<br>

### **The conflict over the common gene pool**

Alleles that increase fitness in one sex can reduce fitness in the other because males and females often experience discordant selection on shared traits (including expression traits!). The evolution of sexual dimorphism suggests that some conflict can be resolved through sex-specific adaptation, but conflict may persist when shared genetic architecture prevents traits from evolving independently in each sex.
- How often does sexual conflict persist despite sexual dimorphism? 
- What factors cause some traits to experience prolonged sexual conflict compared to others? 
- Does the extent of sexual conflict vary across environmental contexts (e.g., benign vs. novel conditions)?
